**Read-outs (revised for magnet-in-push regime):**

1. **All three stages are genuinely over-driven by τ.** With a neodymium magnet (no iron loading), `τ_eff ≈ τ_air`, so the table values are the real deal. The 1–3 τ sweet-spot maxima are **3804 / 1317 / 1125 µs**; current pulses are **2.1× / 5.3× / 4.7× longer**. After 3τ the field is at >95 % of steady state, so the extra on-time is delivering essentially zero additional B while continuing to dump I²R heat into the winding and prolonging the field after the magnet has exited the gradient.
2. **Push-mode force is monotonically forward, with `1/r⁴` falloff — no "back-pull."** Earlier I argued that residual coil current after the magnet exits exerts a backwards force on the magnet's trailing pole. **That was wrong** (corrected per operator pushback, 2026-04-26). The on-axis field `B_z(z)` is single-signed and peaked at coil centre; for an anti-parallel magnet (push), `F_z = ∇(m·B) = -|m| · ∂B_z/∂z`. On the +z side of centre, `∂B_z/∂z < 0`, so `F_z > 0` (forward) and stays forward as long as the coil is energised — *and* during flyback decay, since Lenz preserves current direction → field direction → force direction. Leaving the coil energised "a long time" therefore gives **diminishing returns (force ∝ 1/r⁴ as the magnet moves away), not negative returns.**
3. **So why prefer shorter pulses in push mode? Heat and shared-bank energy economy, not force direction.**
   - **Heat.** At 3τ the field is at >95 % saturation; extra on-time is mostly I²R loss into the winding.
   - **Shared-rail/bank economy.** With `rail_source_active = 0` across this dataset (legacy / shared-bank regime per project context), each coil's pulse pulls from a common reservoir. Energy not consumed by coil 1 is still available to keep V_rail higher when coil 2 fires, and ditto for coil 3. **Down-range stages are paid for with energy left on the table by upstream stages.** This is the operator's framing, and it's the dominant practical reason to find each coil's *shortest pulse with no velocity penalty* rather than a "free lunch."
4. **Brake-resistor sizing — retracted as a velocity lever.** Faster flyback decay means *less* total integrated forward impulse during the decay tail, not more, since the decay-phase force is in the same direction as the pulse-phase force. Higher brake-R may still be desirable for thermal margin or EMI, but it's not a velocity knob in push mode and is dropped from the test plan. (V_CE-spike concerns still apply if any future sweep includes higher brake-R.)
5. **Why might `coil_1_pulse = 8100 > 5100` still have helped?** With the iron-τ rationale gone *and* the tail-pull rationale retracted, two candidates remain: (a) slow stage-1 velocity (sub-1 m/s) means the magnet barely moves through the field region during the 5100 µs pulse — the longer pulse keeps the field alive through more of the magnet's transit; (b) noise on a two-point sweep. Plan B's coil-1 sub-sweep should disambiguate.
6. **Coil 3 is current-starved.** I_peak = 3.9 A is sharply lower than coils 1–2 (15.1 / 10.9 A). With a magnet projectile, force ∝ m_dipole × ∇B, so what matters is the B-field gradient at the magnet's position. Coil 3's higher inductance (more turns × more flux/amp) partially compensates — but only partially. **A thicker-wire rewind on coil 3 is plausibly the largest single velocity lever available.**
7. **Coil ratings did not vary across the dataset.** Cannot disentangle raw-µs vs pulse/τ scaling from this dataset alone.

### Speculative ideal pulse durations (push-mode, magnet projectile, shared-bank regime)

These are best-guesses, not data-backed recommendations — caveat upfront because three things I don't know dominate the answer: exact coil geometry (L_coil, R_coil), the magnet's dipole moment and length, and the bank's actual sag profile under load.

**Working model:**
- Force on dipole magnet at distance `r` past coil centre falls as `1/r⁴` (far-field). Drops to 10 % of peak at `r ≈ 1.78 × r_peak`, where `r_peak` (the on-axis position of the gradient peak) is roughly `L_coil/2 + R_coil/2`, i.e. just past the downstream coil end.
- Useful "field-must-be-on" window from fire time = (transit through the >10%-force zone past midpoint) + (current ramp ≈ 3τ to saturate).
- Rough working number for the >10% zone width past midpoint: `d_eff ≈ L_coil + R_coil`. Assuming a typical pancake-ish stage coil with `L_coil ≈ 15 mm, R_coil ≈ 7 mm`, `d_eff ≈ 22 mm`. Sensitivity to this assumption is large — adjust per actual measurements.

| Stage | Avg v through coil (m/s) | Transit `d_eff/v` (µs) | Ramp `3τ` (µs) | **Best-guess ideal pulse (µs)** | Current pulse (µs) | Comment |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Coil 1 | ~0.5 (0 → ~1)   | ~44 000 | 3804 | **~5500–7000** (cap-energy-limited, NOT transit-limited) | 8100 | Mild over-drive; current 8100 is plausibly fine but the upper end of the band. |
| Coil 2 | ~4 (3.5 → 4.5) | ~5500 | 1317 | **~5000** (3τ_ramp + 1×transit, ~5500–6000 with safety) | 6960 | ~15–25 % over. Test 4000–5500. |
| Coil 3 | ~6 (4.5 → 7)   | ~3700 | 1125 | **~3500–4000** | 5300 | ~30 % over. Test 2500–4000. |

**Coil 1 special case:** at sub-1 m/s, the magnet's transit through `d_eff` takes tens of milliseconds. The pulse cannot remain saturated that long because the bank drains: with shared-bank `RC ≈ 4.1 Ω × 12 000 µF ≈ 49 ms` (or much less if bank is smaller), the current is well into the decay regime by then. So coil 1's optimal pulse is set by *where the cap-bank energy budget runs out*, not by transit time. Operator-configurable trade-off: spend more on coil 1 (more impulse to a slow magnet) at the cost of less V_rail for coils 2–3.

If a velocity model existed for the magnet through coil 1, this could be optimised more precisely. As a hand-wavy split: with a ~23 J shared budget and the magnet's KE at the muzzle currently ~25 mJ at 7 m/s with ~1 g effective mass — the energy *delivered* to the magnet is a small fraction of cap energy, so the bank rationing is mostly about which stage gets to fire at high V_rail. Top-of-bank current matters most for coil 1 (no momentum yet to lose) and for coil 3 (high R, limited I_peak headroom).

**Net speculation:** total energy savings from shortening coils 2 and 3 to my best guesses (≈ 1.5 J on coil 2 + 0.6 J on coil 3 ≈ 2 J reclaimed) shows up as ~10 % less bank sag during stages 2 and 3, which *might* show up as ~few-percent velocity gain on coil 3. **Confidence: Low.** The signal could easily be inside the rig's noise floor.

### Where should the magnet be at the rising edge of the pulse?

User's naive guess: 51 % (just past midpoint). My answer: **51 % is approximately right for stage 2 and slightly late for stage 3; for stage 1 the magnet should be considerably further past midpoint, closer to 80–95 %.** Reasoning:

The on-axis gradient `∂B_z/∂z` peaks roughly at the *downstream coil-end face* (z = +L_coil/2 from centre, i.e. ~100 % of axial length using "0 % = entry face, 100 % = exit face"). Force on the magnet peaks where the gradient peaks. We want the *saturated* field (not the still-ramping one) to be doing the work in that high-gradient zone. So:

```
z_fire = z_peak − v × 3τ
```
Translate to % of axial length, assuming `L_coil ≈ 15 mm` and `z_peak` at the exit face (100 %):

| Stage | v at fire (m/s) | 3τ (µs) | v × 3τ (mm) | z_fire (mm from exit face) | **% of axial length at fire** |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Coil 1 | ~0.3 (just-launched) | 3804 | 1.1 | exit − 1.1 mm | **~93 %** |
| Coil 2 | ~3.5             | 1317 | 4.6 | exit − 4.6 mm | **~69 %** |
| Coil 3 | ~5.5             | 1125 | 6.2 | exit − 6.2 mm | **~59 %** |

These shift firmly toward the *late* side of midpoint, **not toward 51 %** — because the gradient peak is at the coil end, not at midpoint. Midpoint is the *zero-force* point (by symmetry); firing there means the field starts ramping at exactly the moment force-per-amp is least. The naive 51 % rule is roughly correct only in the limit where the coil is very short relative to the magnet's transit during ramp time — which is the case for fast stages and fast-ramping coils, but not stage 1.

**Caveat:** if the coil is more pancake-shaped (`L_coil < R_coil`), the gradient peak shifts toward `r ≈ R_coil/2` past centre — closer to midpoint in axial-% terms. For a square aspect ratio (L=2R) it's roughly at the coil end as I assumed. **Ground-truth this against the actual coil dimensions and a Hall-probe or sim before betting much on it.**
