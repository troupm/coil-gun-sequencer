### Why the new sequence regressed (diagnostic)

The operator's new sequence intentionally explored parameter regions that the top-quartile profile now identifies as harmful: long delays, rail-on, cap bank 4000. The CV improvement (24% ‚Üí 16%) suggests the rig procedure got *more consistent*, not that the parameter space got *better*. The combination ‚Äî reliable measurements of a worse regime ‚Äî is actually the ideal diagnostic outcome, because the signal/noise ratio on "X hurts" is now excellent.

**Physics synthesis of the regressions:**

- **Cap 4000 ¬µF hurt because the pulse-profile stayed the same.** At fixed coil-pulse duration_us, a 4√ó larger cap holds the rail voltage up longer, so coil current stays near peak for longer ‚Äî which means the back-EMF from the coil pulls the projectile *backward* for longer after it's past the coil center. More total energy delivered, but less of it in the right direction.
- **Long delays hurt for the same reason, worse.** A delay of 15000 ¬µs means coil 2 fires while the projectile is already ‚Öî of the way to gate 2. Coil 2's field then *pulls the projectile toward coil 2's center*, which is behind the projectile at that point ‚Äî direct deceleration.
- **Rail-on hurt, co-varying.** Continuous rail voltage on a capacitive-discharge supply doesn't just add energy; it extends the current-on time the same way a bigger cap does. Same back-EMF failure mode.

**The good news:** all three failure modes share a root cause ‚Äî pulse envelope extends past the projectile's optimal acceleration window. Fixing that (proportionally shorter coil pulses, or projectile-position-locked turn-off) opens the door to using the larger hardware productively. **That's the interesting experiment to run next.**
